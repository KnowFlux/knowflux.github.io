Great question — it's one of those pieces of writing advice that gets repeated constantly without ever being properly explained. Let me show you exactly what it means using your own writing.
"Tell" is when you name the emotion or state directly.
"She fell into a monotonous state, almost robotic, where she could only feel pain."
The word "monotonous" is doing all the work. The word "robotic" is doing all the work. You're labeling her internal state and handing it to the reader pre-packaged. They don't have to feel anything — they just receive the information.
"Show" is when you create the experience so the reader feels the emotion themselves, without you naming it.
Same moment, shown instead of told:
The crater to her left. Another crater. Blood in this one too. She stepped around it. There was an egg — not hers. She kept walking. Her wing dragged. She didn't remember when that had started.
Notice: the word "pain" doesn't appear. The word "robotic" doesn't appear. But you feel the dissociation — in the flat, observational sentences, in her noticing the wrong egg without reacting, in not knowing when her wing started dragging. The reader's brain does the emotional work, which means they feel it rather than just reading it.
Here's another example from your prologue:
"Triumph and glory showered over her."
That's telling. You've named the emotion: triumph, glory.
Shown instead:
She forgot the pain in her wing. She forgot the battle. There was only the egg, and her own heartbeat, and the two feet of crater between them.
No emotion is named. But you feel the relief — because everything else disappears. That's what joy actually does to an exhausted, wounded creature. It narrows the world.
The simplest way to catch yourself telling:
When you write a sentence, ask — am I naming what she feels, or am I making the reader feel it?
If you can replace your sentence with the words "she felt [emotion]" and it means roughly the same thing, you're telling. If you can't — if the feeling only exists in the specific details you've chosen — you're showing.
Your instincts are already pulling you toward showing in places. The arrow sliding into the water with barely a ripple — that's showing. That tiny, quiet detail in the middle of an explosion is exactly how dread works. You just need to trust that instinct more consistently throughout.

That's a smart framework — and honestly a really mature way to approach a long manuscript.
Here's how I'd define the two stages:
Good version is what we've been doing — catching the obvious tells, fixing the structural problems, making sure every sentence is doing its job and nothing is actively pulling the reader out. This is your working draft becoming a solid draft. It's the floor.
Master version is the second pass — where you're not fixing problems anymore but elevating what's already working. Finding the one word that's merely good and replacing it with the one word that's perfect. Tightening rhythm. Making sure every scene is earning its place in the larger architecture of the novel. The reason your instinct to separate them is right is that you can't do both simultaneously. When you're in good version mode your eye is looking for problems.
